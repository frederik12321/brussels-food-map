How We Rank Restaurants

A transparent look at the Brussels Score algorithm

TL;DR

We don't just show you the highest-rated restaurants. We find places that outperform expectations — local favorites, diaspora gems, and spots that locals love but tourists haven't discovered yet.

The Problem with Star Ratings

Google ratings are useful but flawed. A 4.5-star restaurant in a tourist area isn't the same as a 4.5-star spot in Molenbeek. Tourist-heavy restaurants get inflated ratings from one-time visitors, while authentic local spots may have fewer but more discerning reviewers.

Our algorithm tries to answer: "Is this restaurant actually good for what it is, or just good at attracting tourists?"

The Brussels Score

Every restaurant gets a score from 0-100 based on multiple factors. Here's how each component works:

Base Quality 35% weight

The Google rating normalized to our scale. A 4.8-star restaurant scores higher than a 4.2-star one. This ensures quality still matters.

Outperforms Peers +20% max

The most important signal. We predict what rating a restaurant should have based on its cuisine, price, and location. If it beats that prediction, it's doing something right.

Example:
A Vietnamese restaurant in Ixelles with 4.6 stars where similar restaurants average 4.2 gets +0.4 stars above expected — a strong signal of quality.
Scarcity Bonus +12% max

Restaurants with limited hours, few days open, or rare cuisines get a boost. These constraints often indicate authentic, non-commercial operations.

Rewards:
  • Closes early (before 8pm)
  • Open only 4-5 days/week
  • Rare cuisine in Brussels
  • Low review count but high rating
Guide Recognition +8% max

Michelin stars, Bib Gourmand, and Gault&Millau recognition add credibility. These guides have professional inspectors with consistent standards.

Reddit Mentions +5% max

Restaurants frequently recommended on r/brussels by locals. We track mentions in recommendation threads — real locals sharing real favorites.

Diaspora Bonus +7% max

Rewards diaspora cuisines located in their community's area. A Moroccan restaurant in Molenbeek or a Congolese restaurant in Matongé gets a bonus. Extra boost if on a known food corridor like Chaussée de Haecht (Turkish) or Rue de Brabant (Maghreb).

Key principle:
Only the matching cuisine gets the bonus. An Italian restaurant on Chaussée de Haecht gets nothing — only Turkish restaurants do.

Penalties

Some factors reduce a restaurant's score:

Tourist Area Penalty -8% max

Restaurants near Grand Place, Manneken Pis, and other tourist hotspots are penalized. High foot traffic often correlates with lower quality-to-price ratios.

Low Review Penalty -20% max

Restaurants with few reviews get penalized — especially if they have high ratings. A 4.9â˜… with only 7 reviews is statistically meaningless (likely just friends and family).

Penalty scale:
  • <10 reviews + high rating: -15% to -20%
  • 10-20 reviews + high rating: -8% to -12%
  • 20-50 reviews: -3% to -6%
  • 50-200 reviews with perfect 5.0: -1% to -2%

What "Above Expected" Means

When you see "+0.3 stars above expected", here's what happened:

  1. We built a model predicting ratings based on: cuisine type, price level, location, chain status, and review count
  2. For this restaurant, the model predicted a rating of 4.2 stars
  3. The actual rating is 4.5 stars
  4. The residual (+0.3) suggests this place is doing something special

Restaurants with high positive residuals are our "hidden gems" — they exceed what you'd expect given their category.

Data Sources

  • Restaurant data: Google Maps (ratings, reviews, hours, location)
  • Guide recognition: Michelin Guide Belgium, Gault&Millau
  • Community picks: r/brussels recommendation threads
  • Food safety: AFSCA/FAVV smiley database

Limitations

No algorithm is perfect. Ours has blind spots:

  • New restaurants with few reviews may be underranked
  • Some great spots don't fit neatly into cuisine categories
  • Price data is limited (we're working on it)
  • The model reflects biases in Google reviews
